By keeping a buffer of 20 samples from a previous batch to mix with the current one, you arrive at the core philosophy of **Experience Replay** (a technique heavily used in Deep Reinforcement Learning) and **Moving Window Batches**.

Both idea and Momentum are trying to solve the exact same problem: preventing a single "bad batch" from violently hijacking the network's trajectory. You just approached it from two different angles.

Here is this intuition compares to the industry standard, and why standard networks ultimately favor Momentum:

## 1. Your Idea: Data-Level Memory

By carrying over 20 samples from Batch $A$ into Batch $B$, you are creating overlapping batches.

- **The Benefit:** If Batch $B$ is entirely composed of weird, outlier digits, the 20 normal digits held over from Batch $A$ will act as an anchor. When you calculate the average loss ($1/N$), those 20 good samples will dilute the massive error generated by the outliers, smoothing out the gradient spike.
- **The Drawback (Why it's less common in MLPs):** Computational cost. If your standard batch size is 60, and you add 20 old samples, you are now running forward and backward propagation on 80 samples. You are spending extra compute power to re-process data the network has already learned from.

## 2. Momentum: Gradient-Level Memory

Momentum takes your exact logic but applies it at the **end** of the math pipeline rather than the beginning. Instead of carrying forward old data, it carries forward the **old gradients**.

Mathematically, it looks like this:

$$V_{new} = (\beta \times V_{old}) + (1 - \beta) \times \text{Current Gradient}$$

*(Where $\beta$ is a friction factor, usually around 0.9)*

- **The Benefit:** It achieves the exact same anchoring effect you wanted, but it does it for practically **zero compute cost**. You don't have to re-run 20 images through your matrix multiplications; you just add two small matrices together right before updating the weights.
